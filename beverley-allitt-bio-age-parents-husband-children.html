Beverley Allitt Biography

Born on October 4, 1968, and convicted of murdering four infants, attempting to murder three others, and causing grievous harm to six more at Grantham and Kesteven Hospital in Lincolnshire between February and April 1991, she operated within the confines of the hospital’s children’s ward, wearing the guise of a State Enrolled Nurse.

Infamously, Allitt is said to have administered substantial insulin doses to at least two victims, with a large air bubble discovered in another child’s body. Though a pattern emerged, initial police investigations struggled to fully comprehend the extent of her attacks.

In May 1993, a grave reckoning awaited Allitt as she received thirteen life sentences at Nottingham Crown Court. Justice David Latham, the sentencing judge, declared her a perpetual threat to society, deemed unlikely ever to be considered safe for release. Presently detained at Rampton Secure Hospital in Nottinghamshire, she became eligible for parole upon the expiration of her thirty-year minimum sentence in November 2021.

Allitt’s upbringing saw her attend Charles Read Secondary Modern School, and later, she pursued nursing studies at Grantham College. The dark span of her criminal activities stretched over 59 days, encompassing thirteen children, four of whom tragically did not survive. Following the death of Becky Phillips, medical personnel grew suspicious of the alarming frequency of cardiac arrests within the children’s ward, prompting police intervention.

Charged with four counts of murder, eleven of attempted murder, and eleven of causing grievous harm, Allitt pleaded not guilty to each charge. However, on May 28, 1993, she was found guilty on all counts and sentenced to concurrent life imprisonments, her new home being Rampton Secure Hospital.

In a 2018 documentary, Allitt seemingly revealed her resistance to incarceration before her trial, later requiring transfer to Rampton Secure Hospital. Forensic psychologist Jeremy Coid and criminologist Elizabeth Yardley attested to her mental state, arguing against her hospitalization. Allitt’s admission of guilt in a failed bid to evade prison went undisclosed to victims’ families.

Justice Stanley Burnton confirmed her thirty-year minimum sentence in December 2007. Allitt’s enigmatic motives have never been fully unveiled, with some speculating a connection to factitious disorder or Munchausen syndrome by proxy, where symptoms are fabricated or falsified in those under one’s care for personal attention.

In literature and media, Allitt’s sinister tale found representation in Nick Davies’ “Murder on Ward Four” and a BBC dramatization titled “Angel of Death” (2005), starring Charlie Brooks as Allitt. Her story also featured in true crime documentaries and inspired the Black Sabbath song “The Hand That Rocks The Cradle.”

The name Beverley Allitt endures as a haunting reminder of the chilling reality that lurks behind seemingly ordinary facades, sparking questions of motive, psychology, and the unfathomable depths of human behavior.
